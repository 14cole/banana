================================================================================
2D RCS SOLVER — MATH REFERENCE
================================================================================

This document describes the mathematics of the 2D boundary-integral-equation
radar cross section solver implemented in rcs_solver.py.  Part 1 gives a
plain-English overview aimed at an engineer who wants to understand what the
solver does, why it works, and when to trust it.  Part 2 is the full-depth
mathematical derivation of every formulation that the solver dispatches.


================================================================================
PART 1 — HIGH-LEVEL OVERVIEW
================================================================================

1.1 WHAT THE SOLVER COMPUTES
----------------------------

Given a 2D shape (or collection of shapes) built from line segments, arcs, and
material properties, the solver computes the radar cross section (RCS) — a
measure of how much electromagnetic energy the shape scatters back toward the
radar.

Inputs:
  - A geometry snapshot: line segments + arcs (optionally closed into bodies)
  - Segment types (PEC, dielectric, resistive sheet, IBC-coated PEC, etc.)
  - Material properties (permittivity, permeability, surface impedance)
  - Frequency or frequency sweep
  - Incidence angle(s) and polarization (TE = E-field axial, TM = H-field axial)

Outputs:
  - Monostatic RCS σ(θ) in dB and linear units
  - Per-sample far-field amplitude (real, imaginary, phase)
  - Solve diagnostics: formulation used, residual norm, condition number

The solver reports σ in the physical 2D "scattering width" normalization:

    σ_2D(φ) = |A(φ)|² / (4k)

where A is the far-field amplitude and k is the free-space wavenumber.  Under
this convention, a PEC circular cylinder matches the analytic Mie series
term-for-term (checked to within 0.1 dB for ka from 0.5 to 10).


1.2 WHY THIS APPROACH
---------------------

The solver uses the Boundary Integral Equation (BIE) method, also called the
Method of Moments (MoM), rather than a volumetric method like Finite Element
or Finite Difference Time Domain.  The key reasons:

  * Only the surface of the scatterer needs to be meshed.  A 10λ-wide metallic
    shape needs ~200 boundary elements, not ~40 000 volumetric cells.
  * The radiation condition is satisfied exactly by the Green's function —
    no artificial absorbing boundaries in the computational domain.
  * Far-field evaluation is analytic: once the surface current is known, the
    scattered field at any observation angle is one quadrature away.

The tradeoff: the BIE matrix is dense (every element interacts with every
other via the Green's function), so the storage is O(N²) and dense LU solve
is O(N³).  The FMM acceleration (see SOLVER_METHODS.txt) brings this down to
O(N log N) for large problems.


1.3 THE CORE IDEA IN ONE PARAGRAPH
----------------------------------

Every solution to the 2D Helmholtz equation (∇²u + k²u = 0) outside a body can
be written as an integral over the body's boundary of the product of a
"source density" times the Green's function (or its normal derivative).  The
boundary conditions — PEC is u=0 for TE or ∂u/∂n=0 for TM; resistive sheet is
u = Z_s·J_z; dielectric is continuity of u and (some factor times) ∂u/∂n —
become integral equations for the unknown source density on the boundary.
Discretize the boundary into straight panels, expand the source density in
piecewise-linear basis functions, test against the same basis (Galerkin), and
you get a dense linear system A·x = b.  Solve it (LU or iterative GMRES with
FMM acceleration).  Integrate the solution density against e^{-jkr̂·r'} along
the boundary to get the far-field amplitude A(φ).  Compute σ = |A|²/(4k).


1.4 THE FOUR THINGS THAT MAKE THIS SOLVER WORK
----------------------------------------------

(1) Correct Green's function and sign conventions

The 2D scalar Green's function under the e^{-jωt} time convention is

    G(r,r') = (j/4) H₀^(2)(k|r-r'|)

and ALL the derivations downstream depend on this sign.  Using H₀^(1) or
e^{+jωt} flips signs in unpredictable places.  The solver uses (j/4)H₀^(2)
consistently; the convention check script (convention_check.py) validates
this by reproducing known analytic answers.

(2) Proper handling of the near-singular quadrature

When two panels are close or touching, the kernel G(r,r') diverges
logarithmically.  Naive Gauss-Legendre quadrature gives wrong self and
neighbor interactions by factors of 2 or more.  The solver uses:
  - Duffy transforms for self and touching panels (exact treatment of log)
  - Adaptive recursive subdivision for near-but-not-touching pairs
  - Standard Gauss-Legendre for far pairs (cheap and accurate)

(3) The right formulation for each material type

One-size-fits-all fails.  For each geometry class the solver picks an integral
representation that gives a well-conditioned system:
  - PEC closed body: CFIE (combined field) to avoid interior resonances
  - Single dielectric body: two-density indirect formulation (u and q on same surface)
  - Multi-region or coated: direct coupled trace formulation
  - Resistive sheet: single-layer (TE) or double-layer/hypersingular (TM)
  - Mixed sheet + PEC: unified SLP/DLP with per-node diagonal

(4) Validation at every level

Numerical claims are backed by reference solutions:
  - Mie series for circular cylinders (PEC, dielectric, magnetic, coated)
  - Reciprocity test σ(α→β) = σ(β→α) for bistatic scattering
  - PEC/transparent limits for resistive sheets
  - Stair-step consistency for tapered impedance


1.5 WHAT YOU NEED TO GIVE THE SOLVER
------------------------------------

For the physics to be right, the geometry must satisfy:
  - Closed bodies wound clockwise (produces outward-pointing panel normals)
  - At least ~10 panels per wavelength (20 is safer for tight features)
  - Consistent material assignments (same IBC flag means same impedance)

The solver handles:
  - Multiple disjoint bodies
  - Open strips and sheets
  - Stacked materials (layered dielectric coatings)
  - Tapered IBC along a segment (new feature, see TAPERED_IBC.md)
  - Mixed material types in one geometry, within the dispatcher's routing
    table (see Part 2.10)


1.6 WHEN TO DOUBT A RESULT
--------------------------

  - Residual norm much above 1e-10: system is ill-conditioned or too few
    panels.  Refine the mesh.
  - A closed body whose ka > 5 solved without CFIE: interior resonances
    may contaminate the answer.  Use cfie_alpha=0.2 (default).
  - Very thin layers (< λ/20 thick) near a surface: near-singular quadrature
    may under-resolve.  Use more panels per wavelength.
  - Extreme impedance contrasts (Z_s < 0.01 Ω or > 10⁴ Ω): system
    approaches a limiting case (PEC or transparent) and conditioning
    degrades.  The dedicated limit formulations (pure PEC, no sheet)
    are more accurate.


================================================================================
PART 2 — FULL MATHEMATICAL DERIVATION
================================================================================


2.1 CONVENTIONS
---------------

Time convention:      e^{-jωt}
Wavenumber:           k = ω/c (real and positive for lossless free space)
Free-space impedance: η = √(μ₀/ε₀) ≈ 376.730313668 Ω
Speed of light:       c = 299792458 m/s

Polarization:
  TE: E_z is axial, (H_x, H_y) in-plane.  The scalar unknown u = E_z.
  TM: H_z is axial, (E_x, E_y) in-plane.  The scalar unknown u = H_z.

Panel geometry:
  Each panel has endpoints p₀, p₁, length L = |p₁ - p₀|, tangent t̂ = (p₁-p₀)/L,
  normal n̂ = (-t_y, t_x) (90° rotation of tangent counterclockwise).

  For closed contours wound clockwise, this normal points OUT of the body.
  This is critical — an inward-pointing normal flips the sign of every
  normal-derivative term in the integral equations.  The convention check
  script verifies this on known geometries.


2.2 THE 2D HELMHOLTZ EQUATION AND GREEN'S FUNCTION
---------------------------------------------------

Outside the scatterer, the total field u (= E_z for TE, = H_z for TM) satisfies

    (∇² + k²) u = 0

with the Sommerfeld radiation condition as r → ∞.  The fundamental solution is

    G(r, r') = (j/4) H₀^(2)(k|r - r'|)

Under our e^{-jωt} convention, H₀^(2) — not H₀^(1) — gives outgoing waves.
We will also need:

  ∂G/∂n(r')         = source-side normal derivative
  ∂G/∂n(r)          = observation-side normal derivative (used in hypersingular)

Key operators, acting on a density φ(r') on the boundary Γ:

  (S φ)(r) = ∫_Γ G(r, r') φ(r') dr'            (single-layer potential, SLP)
  (K φ)(r) = ∫_Γ ∂G(r,r')/∂n(r') φ(r') dr'     (double-layer potential, DLP)
  (K' φ)(r) = ∫_Γ ∂G(r,r')/∂n(r) φ(r') dr'    (adjoint double-layer)
  (D φ)(r) = ∫_Γ ∂²G(r,r')/∂n(r)∂n(r') φ(r') dr' (hypersingular)

Jump relations at the boundary (for r approaching Γ from inside/outside):

    S φ:   continuous
    K φ:   lim from +side  =  +½ φ + K φ        (K φ at r ∈ Γ is principal value)
           lim from -side  =  -½ φ + K φ
    ∂n S φ: lim from +side =  -½ φ + K' φ
            lim from -side =  +½ φ + K' φ
    D φ:   continuous (hypersingular, requires Maue identity for practical
                       evaluation, see §2.4)

These jump relations are what turn the integral representation into a
boundary integral equation when we enforce BCs on each side of Γ.


2.3 THE PLANE-WAVE INCIDENT FIELD
---------------------------------

Incident angle φ_inc measured from +x axis, incident direction d̂ =
(cos φ_inc, sin φ_inc).  The incident plane wave is

    u_inc(r) = exp(j k d̂ · r)

Its normal derivative on the boundary:

    ∂u_inc/∂n(r) = j k (d̂ · n̂) exp(j k d̂ · r)

These appear on the right-hand side of every integral equation we solve.


2.4 THE MAUE IDENTITY (HYPERSINGULAR OPERATOR)
----------------------------------------------

Evaluating D directly requires integration of a 1/r² kernel, which has
O(1/h) error for smooth integrands and is unstable.  The Maue identity
expresses D in terms of S only, dodging the hypersingular quadrature:

For a single element pair (obs, src), the hypersingular block is

    D_{obs,src} = -k² (n_obs · n_src) S_{obs,src}_scalar
                 + (t_obs ⊗ t_src) / (L_obs L_src) · Σ S_{obs,src}

where S_{obs,src} is the single-layer block between the two elements, and
"Σ S" is the sum of S evaluated with constant testing functions.  This is
what _assemble_linear_hypersingular_matrix implements.  The derivation
comes from integration by parts on the tangential derivatives:

    (D φ)(r) = ∫ ∂²G/∂n∂n' φ dr'
             = ∂_t [∫ G (∂_t' φ) dr'] - k² (n · n') ∫ G φ dr'

(with boundary terms assumed to vanish on closed contours; for open contours
the Meixner edge condition pins the relevant endpoint values).


2.5 TE PEC FORMULATION (EFIE — ELECTRIC FIELD INTEGRAL EQUATION)
----------------------------------------------------------------

For TE polarization on a PEC body, the axial electric field E_z must
vanish on the surface.  Represent the scattered field by a single-layer
potential:

    u_s(r) = (S σ)(r)

for an unknown density σ.  The total field on Γ is u_inc + u_s.  BC:

    u_inc + u_s = 0  on Γ
  ⇒ S σ = -u_inc

In Galerkin form with piecewise-linear basis φ_j:

    [S_ij] σ = -[<φ_i, u_inc>]

where S_ij = <φ_i, S φ_j>.  Single density, single equation per node.

Far field: u_s(r,φ) ~ (phase) A(φ) as r → ∞, with

    A(φ) = ∫_Γ σ(r') exp(j k d̂_obs · r') dr'

(standard SLP far-field projector).


2.6 TM PEC FORMULATION (MFIE — MAGNETIC FIELD INTEGRAL EQUATION)
----------------------------------------------------------------

For TM polarization on a PEC body, ∂H_z/∂n on the surface equals the surface
current J_tan via ∇ × H = J.  Representing u_s = S σ as before, the BC is

    ∂(u_inc + u_s)/∂n = 0  on Γ

Taking the normal derivative of S σ and using the jump relation:

    ∂_n(S σ) = -½ σ + K' σ   (from the +side, exterior)
  ⇒ (-½ M + K') σ = -∂u_inc/∂n

where M is the boundary mass matrix from Galerkin testing.  This is the
classical MFIE.

In the solver, this appears as _solve_tm_robin_mfie with zero Robin impedance.


2.7 TE/TM IBC FORMULATION (GENERALIZED ROBIN BC)
-------------------------------------------------

Surface Impedance Boundary Condition (SIBC), also called Leontovich or Robin BC:

    TE: E_z = Z_s · J_tan  where J_tan = ∂(E_z)/∂n / (jωμ)  (unit factors)
    TM: E_tan = Z_s · J_axial, with E_tan derived from H_z

For TE this reduces (after dimensional normalization) to

    u + α (∂u/∂n) = 0    where  α = Z_s / (jkη)    [TE Robin coefficient]

For TM the analogous combination after unit conversion is

    u + α (∂u/∂n) = 0    where  α = jkη Z_s / k²   ≈ factors (see §2.9)

The unified Robin-BIE representation (u_s = S σ, σ is the unknown SLP
density):

  Apply BC:  (u_inc + S σ) + α ∂_n(u_inc + S σ) = 0
  Jump:      ∂_n(S σ)|_+ = -½ σ + K' σ
  ⇒ system:  (S + α(-½M + K')) σ = -u_inc - α ∂u_inc/∂n

This is _solve_robin_bie (TE) and its TM variant.  The dispatcher picks
per-node α from each element's Z_s, averaged over incident elements at
shared nodes (this is how the tapered-IBC feature works — Z_s varies with
the panel's arc-length position, and the α array is correspondingly
per-node).


2.8 DIELECTRIC TRANSMISSION FORMULATION (TWO-DENSITY INDIRECT)
--------------------------------------------------------------

For a single dielectric body (TYPE 3), the BCs are transmission:

    u^+ = u^-                         (u continuous across Γ)
    (1/ξ⁺) ∂u^+/∂n = (1/ξ⁻) ∂u^-/∂n   (normal flux continuous;
                                       ξ = μ for TE, ε for TM)

where +/- are exterior/interior.  Represent the exterior and interior
scattered fields separately:

    Exterior:  u_s^+ = S^+ σ + ½ M σ + K^+ σ  (?)   — actually the solver uses
                                                      an indirect formulation
                                                      with two independent
                                                      densities, μ (exterior
                                                      DLP) and σ (interior SLP)

The two-density system the solver assembles:

    [  ½M + K₀         -S_int      ] [ μ ]     [  -u_inc        ]
    [                               ] [   ]  =  [                ]
    [  D₀              factor·(½M + K'_int) ] [ σ ]   [ -∂u_inc/∂n  ]

with factor = ξ_ext / ξ_int.  The derivation:

  - Enforce u continuity across Γ → first row
  - Enforce (1/ξ) ∂u/∂n continuity → second row
  - D is the exterior hypersingular, computed via Maue (§2.4)
  - S_int, K'_int use the interior wavenumber k_int = k₀ n, where n = √(ε_r μ_r)

This is _solve_dielectric_indirect.  For TE the factor uses μ; for TM it uses ε.


2.9 TYPE 1 SHEET FORMULATIONS (NEWLY DERIVED)
---------------------------------------------

A "sheet" is a thin resistive/reactive layer in free space (both sides are air),
with a jump condition tied to surface admittance Y_s = 1/Z_s.

TE SHEET (E_z axial, J_z current along axis):

Physics:
  - E_z continuous across sheet (tangential E field)
  - Ohm's law: J_z = E_z / Z_s on the sheet
  - Scattered field from a z-directed axial surface current:
        u_s(r) = jkη ∫_Γ G(r, r') J_z(r') dr'

SIBC on the sheet:
        u_inc + u_s = Z_s · J_z

Introduce σ = jkη · J_z so that u_s = SLP(σ) reuses the existing SLP
far-field projector:

        u_inc + S σ = (Z_s / jkη) · σ    (evaluated on Γ)

Galerkin system:
        (S - (Z_s/jkη) M) σ = -<φ, u_inc>

Limit checks:
        Z_s → 0:   S σ = -u_inc              (reduces to TE-PEC EFIE ✓)
        Z_s → ∞:   σ → 0                    (transparent sheet ✓)

This is _solve_te_sheet.  Far field via standard SLP projector.

TM SHEET (H_z axial, tangential current K):

Physics:
  - E_tan continuous across sheet
  - [H_z]+ - [H_z]- = K  (H jump = surface tangential current)
  - Ohm's law: K = E_tan / Z_s
  - E_x = -(1/jωε₀) ∂H_z/∂y  (Maxwell–Ampère on the H_z equation)

Represent u_s by a double-layer potential with density μ = K:

        u_s(r) = ∫_Γ ∂G(r,r')/∂n(r') μ(r') dr'

Properties:
  - u_s jumps by μ across Γ (matches the physical [H_z] = K jump ✓)
  - ∂u_s/∂n is continuous across Γ and equals (D μ)(r)

Relating everything through Maxwell's equations and Ohm's law:

        q = q_inc + (D μ)(r)    where q = ∂u/∂n on Γ
        E_tan = -(1/jωε₀) q
        K = μ = Y_s · E_tan = -(Y_s / jωε₀) q

Substituting q and using jωε₀ = jk/η:

        μ = -(1/(jωε₀ Z_s)) · (q_inc + D μ)
        (jωε₀ Z_s) μ + D μ = -q_inc
        (D + (jk/η) Z_s · I) μ = -q_inc

In Galerkin form (and using Maue for D):

        (N + (jk/η) Z_s M) μ = -<φ, ∂u_inc/∂n>

where N is the Galerkin hypersingular matrix from Maue.

Limit checks:
        Z_s → 0:   N μ = -q_inc              (reduces to TM-PEC Neumann ✓)
        Z_s → ∞:   μ → 0                    (transparent ✓)

MEIXNER EDGE CONDITION (TM only, crucial for open sheets):

At an open strip's endpoints, H_z must be continuous (there's no material
there to support a current jump), so

        K = [H_z]+ - [H_z]- → 0 at free endpoints
        μ → 0 at the geometric endpoints of the sheet

In the solver this is enforced by pinning the rows corresponding to
geometric endpoint nodes: replace the hypersingular row with identity,
set RHS to 0.  The subtlety: the interface-aware mesh builder may create
multiple node IDs at the same geometric location when adjacent segments
have different material signatures (e.g., stair-stepped tapered IBC), so
the pin must be by GEOMETRIC KEY, not by per-node incidence count.

TE does NOT need this pin because σ = jkη · J_z has no Meixner zero at a
resistive-strip edge; the current density can be finite there.

Far field for TM sheet: DLP projector
        A(φ) = ∫_Γ jk (d̂ · n̂) μ(r') exp(jk d̂ · r') dr'


2.10 MIXED SHEET + PEC FORMULATION (UNIFIED REPRESENTATION)
-----------------------------------------------------------

A geometry with both TYPE 1 sheets and pure-PEC TYPE 2 bodies can be handled
in a single block because both surface types admit the same integral
representation:

  - PEC SLP-EFIE (TE):   S σ = -u_inc
  - Sheet TE:            (S - (Z_s/jkη) M) σ = -u_inc
  - Both reduce to:      (S - α_i M) σ = -u_inc  with α_i = Z_i/(jkη) per node,
                         where α_i = 0 on PEC nodes, α_i = Z_s/(jkη) on sheet nodes

Similarly for TM:
  - PEC MFIE:            -½ M σ + K' σ = -q_inc
  - Sheet TM:            (N + (jk/η) Z_s M) μ = -q_inc
  - Note these use DIFFERENT representations (SLP vs DLP).

For TM mixed, the solver uses the DLP representation throughout (μ density),
and the PEC-TM Neumann BC becomes (N μ = -q_inc) — same as the Z → 0 limit
of the sheet formula, with the Meixner pin skipped on closed-body nodes.

The implementation (_solve_mixed_sheet_pec) builds one system with:
  - TE: (S - diag(α_i) · M) σ = -<φ, u_inc>, α_i = Z_i/(jkη) (0 on PEC)
  - TM: (N + diag(α_i) · M) μ = -<φ, q_inc>, α_i = (jk/η)·Z_i (0 on PEC)
  - Meixner pin only at GEOMETRIC sheet endpoints (via
    _geometric_sheet_endpoint_nodes), NOT at closed-PEC nodes

Reciprocity (σ(α→β) = σ(β→α)) and monostatic/bistatic consistency are
satisfied to machine precision (validated: 0.0000 dB error on both checks).


2.11 COUPLED TRACE FORMULATION (GENERAL CASE)
---------------------------------------------

For geometries mixing PEC, IBC, and dielectric interfaces, the solver uses
a direct formulation with two nodal unknowns per node: u (the trace) and
q (the normal flux from the minus side).  The system is built from
representation-formula rows (exterior radiation condition on the + side)
plus BC rows (enforcing each material's condition).

This is _build_coupled_matrix_linear.  The derivation is lengthy but
amounts to:

  Representation row (for each active region):
        (½M + K₋ - K₊) u + (q+γ · S₊ - S₋) q + q+β S₊ q = ... [RHS: incident]

  BC row depends on bc_kind:
  - PEC-TE: u = 0 at the node
  - PEC-TM: q = 0 at the node
  - Robin:  u + α q = 0
  - Transmission: u^+ = u^-, (1/ξ+) q^+ = (1/ξ-) q^- (enforced via coupling
    between the two regions' representation rows)

Junction constraints handle nodes where three or more material types meet.
This is the "general fallback" — less efficient than the specialized paths
for pure geometries, but handles arbitrary combinations.


2.12 MULTI-REGION INDIRECT FORMULATION (LAYERED COATINGS)
---------------------------------------------------------

For geometries with multiple nested homogeneous regions (e.g., dielectric
coating over a PEC core), the solver uses an indirect SLP formulation per
region, coupled across interfaces.  Each region gets its own SLP density on
its bounding interfaces, and transmission conditions couple adjacent regions.

The layout (see _solve_multi_region_indirect):
  - Each interface contributes one or two DOF blocks (minus side, plus side)
  - PEC interfaces: one-sided (only the air-side SLP density)
  - Transmission interfaces: two-sided, with a β = ξ_plus / ξ_minus factor

Far field: sum of exterior-region SLP densities, projected at observation
angles via the standard SLP projector.  This is what
_farfield_at_angles_slp does.


2.13 CFIE (BURTON-MILLER) REGULARIZATION
-----------------------------------------

Closed-body interior resonances are a well-known pathology: at certain
discrete frequencies (determined by the interior eigenvalues of the
Helmholtz operator), the EFIE matrix becomes singular even though the
physical scattering problem is well-posed.  Burton & Miller's remedy (1971):

    Use EFIE + jα/k · MFIE as the combined equation.

At any eigenvalue of the interior problem, EFIE and MFIE cannot both be
singular (their adjoint structure differs), so the combined equation is
regular for all k.

The solver implementation (cfie_alpha parameter, default 0.2):

    A_CFIE = A_EFIE + (jα/k) A_MFIE_correction

where the MFIE correction adds:
  - u-block:  α_BM · (D_minus - D_plus + K'_plus · γ - ½ M · γ)
  - q-block:  α_BM · (M · (1 - ½ β) - K'_minus + K'_plus · β)

with α_BM = jα/k.  These are applied as additive corrections to the
coupled-trace system rows.  Setting cfie_alpha = 0 disables CFIE (useful
for validation against pure-EFIE or pure-MFIE analytic references).


2.14 BOUNDARY-ELEMENT DISCRETIZATION
------------------------------------

The continuous BIE is discretized via piecewise-linear continuous Galerkin:

  - Mesh: N nodes, N_e elements (straight line segments).  Each element has
    two node IDs at its endpoints.

  - Basis: φ_i(r) is the "hat function" — linear on elements incident to
    node i, zero elsewhere, = 1 at node i.

  - Unknown expansion: σ(r) ≈ Σ_i σ_i φ_i(r), with σ_i the nodal DOFs.

  - Galerkin test: enforce <φ_i, (equation)> = 0 for every test function φ_i.

This produces the N×N (or 2N×2N for coupled formulations) dense matrix A,
and the nodal RHS vector b.

Assembly of one matrix entry (e.g., S_ij) requires a double integral:

    S_ij = ∫∫ G(r,r') φ_i(r) φ_j(r') dr dr'

which breaks into element-pair blocks.  For each (obs_element, src_element)
pair, the integrand's behavior depends on their geometric relationship:

  - Coincident (self-term):     Duffy transform handles log singularity exactly
  - Sharing one vertex:         Touching-Duffy transform
  - Near but disjoint:          Recursive subdivision until smooth at the
                                quadrature scale
  - Well-separated:             Plain Gauss-Legendre 8×8 tensor product

All of this is done in _integrate_linear_pair_* functions.  The far-field
blocks can be computed in bulk via vectorized numpy (_assemble_linear_far_blocks).


2.15 THE TAPERED IBC FEATURE
-----------------------------

A tapered impedance row in the IBCS section has the form

    flag  taper  <kind>  R_start  X_start  R_end  X_end

where <kind> ∈ {linear, cosine, exp}.  The solver:

  (1) Parses this into an ImpedanceTaper dataclass (rcs_solver.py:167).
  (2) Each panel records arc_s_center ∈ [0,1] = position along segment.
  (3) MaterialLibrary.get_impedance(flag, freq, arc_s) returns Z(s).
  (4) Each panel gets its own robin_impedance via Z(panel.arc_s_center).
  (5) Per-node α is averaged across incident elements (for shared-node
      continuity on tapered segments).

Consistency is verified by the stair-step test (test_taper_stairstep_cyl.py):
replacing the continuous taper with N constant sub-segments and checking
that the RCS converges to the tapered answer as N grows.  TM converges to
0.006 dB at N=20; TE to ~0.14 dB at N=50 (O(1/N)).


2.16 FAR-FIELD EVALUATION
--------------------------

Once the boundary density is known, the far-field amplitude A(φ) is a
single quadrature of the density against e^{-jk d̂·r'}:

  SLP projector (σ on Γ):
      A(φ) = ∫_Γ σ(r') exp(j k d̂_obs · r') dr'

  DLP projector (μ on Γ):
      A(φ) = ∫_Γ jk (d̂_obs · n̂) μ(r') exp(j k d̂_obs · r') dr'

These are what _farfield_at_angles_slp and _farfield_at_angles_dlp do.
Both use 8-point Gauss-Legendre per element — more than enough for the
non-oscillatory far-field integrand.

The 2D scattering width (physical normalization) is

    σ_2D(φ) = |A(φ)|² / (4k)

matching the asymptotic behavior

    u_s(r,φ) ~ √(1/(8πkr)) · e^{-j(kr - π/4)} · A(φ)

as r → ∞.  This convention makes the solver output match Mie theory for
the circular cylinder term-by-term, with no k-dependent rescaling.


2.17 VALIDATION MATRIX (WHAT WE ACTUALLY CHECKED)
--------------------------------------------------

Quantitative convergence is demonstrated on:

  * PEC cylinder, TE and TM, ka = 0.5 to 10: all within 0.1 dB of Mie.
  * Dielectric cylinder (ε_r = 4, μ_r = 1), TE and TM: within 0.1 dB.
  * Magnetic dielectric (ε_r = 1, μ_r = 4): within 0.1 dB.
  * Coated PEC (ε_r = 4 coating, PEC core): within 0.15 dB.
  * IBC cylinder, six complex-Z cases, TE and TM: within 0.005 dB
    (exact analytic expression via Mie with Robin BC).
  * TYPE 1 sheet PEC limit (Z → 0): matches TYPE 2 PEC strip within 0.01 dB (TE)
    and 0.2 dB (TM, limited by Meixner-pin edge effect).
  * TYPE 1 sheet transparent limit (Z → 10⁴ Ω): σ → -30 dB (both TE, TM).
  * Tapered IBC stair-step convergence (closed TYPE 2 cylinder):
    TM within 0.006 dB at N=20, TE within 0.14 dB at N=50.
  * Bistatic reciprocity (all supported formulations): < 0.002 dB.
  * Monostatic = bistatic at backscatter (all supported formulations): 0.000 dB.

These are in convention_check.py, validate_mie_quick.py, test_validation.py,
test_sheet_*.py, test_mixed_sheet_pec.py, test_taper_stairstep_cyl.py.
